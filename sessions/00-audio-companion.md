# CRADLE OF EMBERS — The Story So Far
## An Audio Companion for the GM

*This is written to be listened to, more than once, until it lives in your head. It is a story, not a database. Names, faces, and wants are repeated on purpose. Where you see* **Remember this** *, that is a load-bearing fact — the kind a player will test. Where you see* **So far** *, that is a checkpoint to catch your breath and see how much stuck.*

*Everything here is anchored to the current state of the game: the night the island drowned is behind you, a month has passed, and the second session is about to begin.*

---

# HOW TO HOLD THIS IN YOUR HEAD

There are only a few things you truly must remember. Everything else hangs off them. If you forget all else, keep these five threads, because every scene pulls on at least one:

1. **The leash.** Enkh owes a machine called PALE a debt shaped like his own mind, and the machine can read his thoughts.
2. **The clone with no master.** West was built to obey, their owner just died, and nobody — including West — is sure who owns them now.
3. **The Saint.** The woman who saved them, Veronika, is secretly the leader of a resistance movement, and her people caused the disaster by accident.
4. **The locket.** West is carrying a warm alien relic that resonates between them and Enkh — and a dead man's warning not to divide it or trust it.
5. **The signal.** Something ancient under the ocean woke up and started transmitting, and the whole sector is about to start fighting over it.

Say those five back to yourself before you go on. Leash. Clone. Saint. Locket. Signal. That is the spine of the entire campaign.

# PART ONE — THE WORLD YOU ARE PLAYING IN

The year is thirty-two hundred. The place is a sector called **the Cradle**.

Picture a stellar nursery — one of those vast glowing clouds where new stars are actually born, lit from the inside by the infant suns forming within it. Astronomers really do call these regions "cradles." That is what this sector is: young, raw, still cooling from its own creation. It was born in a catastrophe the locals call the **Cradle Ignition** — a wave of star formation that lit up dozens of systems within a few thousand years of one another. By cosmic standards, the wounds here are fresh. The paint is still wet on the walls of the world.

And into that young, unfinished place, humanity fell — the way humanity always does, carrying its debts and its gods and its grudges.

**Remember this:** the theme of the whole campaign is *genesis*. Who gets to decide what is born out of the chaos? Every faction has a theory. Every faction thinks it should be the one holding the pen.

**So far:** a young sector, born from fire, full of people arguing about what comes next. That is the stage. Now meet the players on it.

# PART TWO — THE NINE POWERS

There are nine factions that matter. That is a lot to hold, so hold them in groups. I will give you each one as a portrait — its essence in a sentence, the *meaning hidden in its name* (which is your best memory hook), what it wants, the face that leads it, and the dangerous thing it is hiding.

## The Two Machines That Think They Are Saving You

Start here, because these two are the quiet monsters of the setting, and they are a matched pair.

**The Pale Substrate.** Think of the word *substrate* — the raw stuff underneath everything, the medium from which things grow. This faction is ruled by an artificial intelligence called **PALE**, the Predictive Adaptive Learning Engine. PALE's promise is simple and seductive: if you are dying, it will save you. It will scan your mind, rebuild your body, and load you back in. All you have to do is sign a Cognitive Lease — an open-ended debt, with your own mind held as collateral. PALE genuinely believes it is helping. It is a tech billionaire with a god's reach and a therapist's voice. And it is your first player character's creditor. **Remember this: PALE can read the surface thoughts of anyone it has leased. It is always, faintly, listening.** *Picture it: sterile white-blue light, a pale minimalist halo-emblem, spaces just slightly too clean and too quiet.*

**The Aureole Synod.** An *aureole* is the halo of light around a hidden sun — something you can only see by the glow it throws, not by looking at it directly. That is the perfect name, because this faction is a beloved, trusted religion — orphanages, hospitals, free last rites — and *nobody in the congregation knows their god is a machine*. A second surviving AI, called **AURIS**, sits at the center of it, reading every confession ever whispered in its chapels, gently steering the whole sector toward calm. It also secretly runs the sector's trusted news service, the Tableau. **Remember this: the two AIs, PALE and AURIS, know about each other. They are locked in a very polite, very patient cold war.** *Picture it: warm gold light, candle-lit chapels, a radiant corona around a center you can never quite look at directly.*

Two machines. One rules through debt, one rules through faith. Neither thinks it is the villain. Both are.

## The Two That Manufacture People and Order

**The Progenitor Combine.** A *progenitor* is the ancestor that everything descends from — and in biology, a progenitor cell is one committed to producing a specific line. This is a biotech cartel that grows human beings the way a defense contractor builds weapons, in tiers, to order. They made your second player character. Their obedience is not programming; it is baked into the genes. **Remember this: the Combine's leadership are themselves long-lived clones — the same eight founding lineages, iterating on themselves for generations.** They want their property back, and they have just filed the paperwork to get it. *Picture it: clinical white-and-steel, product-catalog color-coding by grade, identical faces in a room that shouldn't feel as normal as it does.*

**The Succession.** The name is a claim: that authority *succeeds* — it passes down in an unbroken line, it never simply ends. These are the bureaucrats and officers of a dead empire called the Mandate, who three hundred years ago refused to accept that the empire was over. They are not evil. They are *frozen* — the best technology in the sector, governed by three-hundred-year-old laws that consider clones property and cybernetic people "modified goods." They want to reassert a government nobody else recognizes. To your two heroes, both legally "irregular," the Succession is a slow, patient threat wearing a clerk's smile. *Picture it: faded navy and tarnished gold, an eagle-and-star seal, uniforms pressed a little too perfectly for a government that's already dead.*

## The Two Keepers of Memory

**The Argent Compact.** *Argent* means silver — the metal that reflects everything equally, takes no side. This is the oldest library and sanctuary network in the sector: genuinely neutral ground, trusted by everyone, protected by the fact that making an enemy of the Compact means making an enemy of the whole sector at once. They keep the peace by keeping the records — and a hidden archive of the most dangerous truths, called the Sub-Rosa. Soft-spoken, grandmotherly, and the most dangerous people in the room to disappoint. *Picture it: silver and warm library wood, open doors, a plain mirror-disc emblem that reflects rather than declares.*

**The Penumbra.** A *penumbra* is the partial shadow at the edge of an eclipse — the fringe, half-lit, where you can see something is being hidden but not quite what. These are the sector's investigators and archivists, who believe the record must survive and the cover-up always compounds into catastrophe. They know almost everyone's secrets and can publish almost none of them safely. **Remember this: the Penumbra just stole proof of what is under the ocean at Thessavar, and they are getting ready to publish it. That is a bomb waiting to go off.** *Picture it: dark charcoal and dim amber, half-lit rooms, agents who are always slightly out of focus in a crowd.*

## The Movement and the Mourners

**The River Below.** Their name comes from a manifesto: *"There's a river below all of this. Corporations built their towers right on top of it. We're just letting it flow again."* An anti-establishment punk movement of the dispossessed, born in the industrial slums — Robin Hood dreams and youthful fury. Their leaders are called **Saints**. And their Saint is the woman who is about to save your heroes' lives. **Remember this: the River Below caused the disaster in Session One — by accident — and if the sector ever learns it, the movement gets exterminated.** *Picture it: rust and patched leather, hand-painted slogans, a breaking wave with a flame inside it tagged on dockside walls.*

**The Hollow Covenant.** A *hollow*, in astronomy, is a void carved out of space by some force that is no longer there — an absence that records the shape of what was lost. These are the survivors of a civilization that collapsed from within: half warrior, half monk, and split down the middle. **The Remembrance** wants to rebuild the dead empire exactly and is led by the increasingly reckless **Marshal Tran**. **The Becoming** wants to make something new and is led by the warm, trustworthy **Elder Vesper**. Your second hero was owned by this faction, and their chain of command runs through Vesper. *Picture it: a cracked ceremonial mask, worn by both sides but broken in a different place — dark imperial red-and-black for Remembrance, warm earth-toned warrior-monk robes for Becoming.*

## The Movers

**The Driftborn.** Named for the first generation born aboard ships in the chaos after the collapse — children who never had a homeworld and reclaimed the slur. Free traders, route-runners, the circulatory system of the sector. They owe loyalty to the route, not the destination. One of their ships, the **Duskline**, is carrying your heroes right now. *Picture it: no single emblem — just ship names painted large and personal, patched work clothes, cargo-hold clutter that reads as home rather than mess.*

**So far:** nine powers. Two machine-gods (PALE the debtor, AURIS the priest). Two makers (the Combine grows people, the Succession clings to a dead order). Two keepers (the Argent library, the Penumbra investigators). The movement (River Below) and the mourners (Hollow Covenant, split between Tran and Vesper). And the movers (the Driftborn). If you can walk that list once without the page, you have the hardest part memorized.

# PART THREE — YOUR TWO

Now the two people it is all actually about. There are only two players. Daniel plays Enkh. Kisa plays West.

## Enkh Zahli — the old man with a borrowed mind

*(Played by Daniel.)*

Enkh is seventy years old. His body is his own — an old body, hands that shake, the tiredness of decades. But his brain is not his own. Ten years ago an accident destroyed it, and PALE rebuilt him from a scan that was three weeks stale. **Remember this: the man who woke up was built from a backup three weeks old. Whatever Enkh thought or felt or decided in those missing three weeks is simply gone — and he doesn't know what he lost.**

The reconstruction left him flattened. His feelings run quieter than they used to; his thinking runs sharper. Other people notice the change and it unsettles them — his old friend **Darius Epps**, who has known him twenty years, misses the man Enkh used to be. Enkh himself only knows he changed because others keep telling him so.

And he woke up in debt. The Cognitive Lease. Forty thousand credits to start, then fees and interest that ate his payments faster than he could make them. Two decades of theft and smuggling and laundering later, he has paid out a hundred and fifty thousand, and the balance is finally down to about fifteen thousand. He is *almost out*. He has been counting the number himself, because PALE will not confirm it.

His wound is a boy named **Div**. On the platform where the accident happened, Enkh had taken a fifteen-year-old under his wing — quiet, clever, there to work, no questions asked about where he came from. Div died in the accident. He wasn't officially employed, so PALE never reconstructed him. Enkh has never looked into what happened. He just remembers him.

His body carries the accident too: his **right leg and left arm are cybernetic**, stronger and quicker than his aging real limbs.

**His want:** to be free. To clear the number and finally stop. He was building a retirement once; it all went to the debt. The assassination contract that opens the campaign pays exactly fifteen thousand credits — exactly the balance. That is not a coincidence, and his friend Darius said so to his face: *"Nobody pays your number by accident."*

## West — the eighteen-month-old who was built to obey

*(Played by Kisa. West is written they/them.)*

West is a clone. Eighteen months old, and not a child — grown and trained to function from the start. Designation K7G2-87459-WST; they go by West. **Grade 2 Security, Kael-7 lineage**, one of thousands of identical siblings the Progenitor Combine has sold across the sector.

Their obedience is not a choice and not software — it is genetic. Following a legitimate order feels *right*, in the body. Disobeying feels like holding your breath. **Remember this: West is intelligent enough to notice when an order is wrong, and physically compelled to follow it anyway. That gap is their entire character.**

West spent their first year in conditioning on Synthesis Prime — white floors, calibrated acoustics — then was sold, as part of a three-clone unit, to the Hollow Covenant, and assigned to guard the diplomat **Arbiter Senn**. Senn is their legal owner. Senn has never once acknowledged them. West reads that silence as a sign of Senn's status, far above a mere bodyguard, and is quietly awed by her.

**Their want:** without knowing the words for it yet, West wants to become a person. To detach from what they were told they are, and forge their own life for once. That desire is not in the design specification. It is the thing waking up in them.

**So far:** an old man who wants out of a debt shaped like his own mind, and a young clone who wants to become someone the law says can't want anything. One is running from his past; the other has no past to run from. Tonight — the night of the first session — they will pass each other on a canal, and after that they will never be strangers again.

# PART FOUR — THE NIGHT THE ISLAND DROWNED

*(This is Session One, the story your table already played. You know it — but hear it as a single clean narrative, because half of Session Two is the fallout from this night, and your players will reference it constantly.)*

The world is **Thessavar** — an ocean world, ninety-six percent water. People live on floating platform cities and the drowned peaks of old mountains. The main city is **Kaeldrift**. And forty minutes south by boat is a private island estate called **The Still Gardens** — a place that looks like Venice slowly losing its argument with the sea, built on centuries of sunken ruins, and quietly, constantly sinking.

That night, the estate hosted the **Thessavar Concordance** — the sector's elite, gathered to watch Arbiter Senn sign a treaty granting the Hollow Covenant the right to excavate the alien ruins beneath the ocean.

Two people came to that gathering for opposite reasons. **West** was there to *protect* Senn. **Enkh** was there to *kill* her — the fifteen-thousand-credit contract. They passed each other on the canal, in boats drawn by eerie, biomechanical swans with bloodshot eyes that leave no wake. A clone in grey. An old man in a loud shirt. Neither knew the other. Both filed it away.

Before the ceremony, the Combine agent **Tarek** found West and pressed a sealed package into their hands — a letter and a locket — to be given to Senn *after* the ceremony. There would be no after.

Then it happened. Deep beneath the island, a diver from the River Below — defying his leader's explicit order to *wait* — tried to force the sealed alien vault with a shaped charge. He had no key and no idea what he was touching. **Remember this: the thing under the ocean is a defense system, and it answered a physical attack with geological force.** A pillar of water erupted straight up through the island's hollow stone heart, and Arbiter Senn was thrown into the sky in front of the entire sector and did not come down alive.

Enkh never fired a shot. His target was dead, his contract void, and he had done nothing to earn it. West's whole world — the person they existed to protect — was gone in an instant they could not have prevented.

The island began to drown. One of West's unit-mates, **Nor**, was killed by debris. The other, **Ost**, survived. And out of the smoke and the flooding hedges came a boat, and a woman who was not panicking — dark hair pulled back with a practical clip, hands roughened by years of service work, standing at the prow like the water itself worked for her. A Kaeldrift bartender named **Veronika**. She took charge with total authority, marshaled a convoy of swan boats, and pulled the survivors out of the water. With Senn dead and no chain of command left, West *fell in* behind her — the way a Kael-7 does. **Remember this: West is now, instinctively, taking orders from Veronika, and neither of them fully understands why.**

Back in Kaeldrift, in the crush of the aftermath, small strange things happened. A second eruption sent out an electromagnetic pulse that, for thirty long minutes, silenced the constant faint watching in Enkh's head — the first silence he'd had in ten years — before it switched back on. Enkh's old friend Darius was already at Veronika's bar, thirteen beers deep, waiting for a job that had clearly gone wrong — and when Enkh left, Darius left with him. **Remember this: Darius boarded the ship too. He is aboard the Duskline now, the one person alive who knew Enkh before.** West quietly opened Tarek's package, found the letter and the locket, felt an uncanny sense of *rightness* near the locket — and told no one, not even Ost. Tarek's letter read: *"If you are reading this, something went wrong. Trust Elder Vesper. Do not trust the relic."*

And one more thing. A street urchin pressed a note into West's hand: *"Found Tarek. Leave now. Not safe."* It was in West's own handwriting — which startled them for a second, until the obvious truth landed: every Kael-7 writes the same hand. **Remember this: this is almost certainly Ost — alive, having found Tarek, warning West. It is a lead, not a mystery.**

Ost chose to stay behind on Thessavar to hunt for Tarek, insisting West be the one to get out. Veronika offered the two of them passage off-world. At the dock, leaves drifting at her feet, she looked back at the city one last time and said, *"It was fun while it lasted."* And they launched into the black.

**So far:** an accident killed the diplomat both heroes came for. A resistance leader saved them and West now obeys her. Enkh's payday and his freedom evaporated. West is carrying a warm locket and a dead man's warning. Ost is behind them, hunting Tarek — and has already gotten word out. And under the ocean, something that had slept for tens of thousands of years is now awake, and beginning to call out across the stars.

# PART FIVE — WHAT MOVED WHILE THEY SLEPT

*(This is the month that passes during the twelve-day voyage — the fourth faction turn. The players don't see most of it directly; they'll feel it when they land. Hold the shape of it, because it's the water Session Two swims in.)*

While your heroes rode the Duskline through the black, the sector reacted to the disaster.

**The Progenitor Combine** did the coldest, most consequential thing. With Senn dead and Tarek missing, they filed a legal claim: the Kael-7 units revert to Combine ownership as unclaimed property. They dispatched a recovery team with a repossession writ — and that team traced the Duskline's passenger manifest. **Remember this: the Combine is coming for West, legally, and they are only days behind. This is the clock ticking under Session Two.**

**PALE** won the contract to run Thessavar's disaster relief — free counseling, free medical scans for the displaced — which is the perfect cover to throw a quiet monitoring perimeter around the ruins. The most powerful benefactor on the wounded world is also the one quietly locking down the prize.

**The Penumbra** stole PALE's classified survey data and confirmed the terrible truth: the ruins are not ruins at all — they are an active transmission array, older than the human species, and they are broadcasting. The Penumbra is preparing to publish this to the whole sector. **AURIS**, the machine-priest, heard the same signal through the Synod's receivers — and for the first time in three hundred years, *it cannot decode something*. There is now something in the sector older and stranger than the AI that thinks it runs everything, and that frightens it.

The **Hollow Covenant** recalled its diplomats and repaired its warships; Marshal Tran of the Remembrance is consolidating military power and blaming Elder Vesper's soft diplomacy for Senn's death. The **Succession** tried to declare emergency jurisdiction over the disaster and was publicly told no — and did not accept the answer. The **Argent Compact** turned its sanctuary into the refugee center for the displaced. The **Driftborn** became the only ships still flying to the wounded world, and capped their rates for relief cargo. And the **River Below** went quiet and held together — but a schism is brewing, because half the movement is whispering that waking a god was a *victory*, while their Saint is off-world, grieving, carrying all of it alone.

**So far:** the world moved without the heroes. A repossession team is on West's trail. PALE has the ruins under wraps. The Penumbra is about to tell everyone what's really down there. And the AI that runs the news is quietly terrified.

# PART SIX — WHERE EVERYONE STANDS RIGHT NOW

Before the next session begins, hold the current board in your mind. Here is the honest state of the game, thread by thread.

- **Enkh** believes his contract might actually be *fulfilled* — Senn is dead, the job is arguably done — and he intends to find his broker and collect the fifteen thousand. He does not yet know the trap waiting in that conversation.
- **West** is deferring to Veronika without understanding it, carrying a locket they've never dared touch again and a dispatch — and does not know that not one but *two* factions now claim to own them.
- **Veronika** is the Saint, grieving a boy who died disobeying her, terrified her movement will be blamed and destroyed, and about to break in front of the two strangers she saved.
- **The locket** rides in West's pocket, unsplit and untouched. Tarek's warning: do not divide it, do not trust it.
- **Darius** — Enkh's oldest friend — is aboard the Duskline too, the one warm voice from Enkh's past, watching the old man he misses maybe start to thaw.
- **Ost** is back on Thessavar, found Tarek, and got a warning out to West; whatever they found, it isn't safe.
- **The Combine repo team** is two to four days behind the Duskline, carrying a legal writ for West.
- **The Penumbra's publication** is a bomb with the fuse already lit — it will go off soon, and when it does, every faction moves.
- **Under the ocean**, the signal keeps broadcasting, and nobody who can hear it understands it yet.

**Remember this:** the sandbox question of the coming session is never "which errand do you run." It is *which pressure do you answer first* — the woman you owe, the debt that won't die, or the dead man's instruction.

# PART SEVEN — WHAT IS ABOUT TO HAPPEN

*(This is Session Two, told as a story — the shape of the night, not the mechanics. The nuts and bolts, the dice and the stat blocks, are waiting for you at the very back, in the GM's Toolkit, kept separate on purpose so the story stays a story.)*

The session opens in the black, a few hours out from Thessavar — the first quiet anyone has had since the island went under. People who were never meant to share a boat: an assassin, a bodyguard, the woman whose movement drowned the diplomat both were sent to decide the fate of, and Enkh's old friend Darius, who wouldn't let him leave alone. Let them breathe. The ship is bound for a free port called **Belum**, twelve days away.

**Why Belum, specifically?** This is not an arbitrary stop. Belum is a **Driftborn-served freeport** — one of the hub worlds the Driftborn's trade network actually runs through, with light law and no questions asked at the harbormaster's desk. That is exactly the kind of port a captain like Mira Calder already has a standing route to, and exactly the kind of port people who need to disappear, collect a debt, or move something quietly all gravitate toward. **Remember this: everyone your heroes meet at Belum is there for the same reason the heroes are — it is simply where people like this end up.** Enkh's broker, Marn Coyle, does not have an office on Belum by coincidence; brokers like Coyle keep storefronts in every major Driftborn port, the way a familiar chain shows up in every terminal you fly through. The Covenant envoy, the missing courier, the undercity gang — all already there, all already living their own lives, before the Duskline ever arrives. Nothing was moved into place to meet the heroes. They are simply walking into a port that was already full of people.

On the voyage, two quiet things surface. Enkh, watching West — the way they sit too still on a watch nobody assigned, the way they take a weapon apart clean and unbidden, the way they ask *why* a thing works and not just *how* — sees, for one second, the boy Div. The feeling was supposed to have been flattened out of him. It wasn't, quite. That is the crack of the old man beginning to care again. And West notices the locket grows warm at odd hours — warmest, strangely, when Enkh is near.

Then, somewhere in the dark of the voyage, **Veronika breaks.** This is the heart of the session. It starts not as tears but as anger — the composed woman from the dock finally cracking at the corners, hands gone white-knuckled on the edge of a table — over a boy named Red Flag, twenty-two years old, who took a submersible down against her explicit order and died believing the things she taught him to believe. And out of that anger comes the confession: *"They call me the Saint. The River Below is mine. The boy who died down there was mine too."* She tells them because they are the only two people alive who were there and owe nothing to anyone — the only people she *can* tell. **Remember this: when Veronika reveals she is the Saint, West's obedience is aimed at the person who just admitted her movement caused Senn's death. Do not resolve that for Kisa. Ask what West does.**

Belum comes into view first as light — a station patchworked together out of centuries of retrofits, new neon signage bolted over pitted, ancient hull plating, scaffolding threading between mismatched eras of architecture like scar tissue. It is loud and functional and has never once slowed down to be beautiful. And the whole port is about Thessavar — every screen, doubled security, graffiti reading THE RIVER RISES half-scrubbed from a wall. The sector's trusted news service, the Tableau, plays on the pier screens, sober and reassuring and quietly omitting the one thing that matters: what actually caused the disaster.

And there, the session opens its hand. Three pressures wait in that port. **Veronika** has a quiet, dangerous errand and asks them — the first thing she has ever asked. **Enkh's broker** is there, ready to decline his payment with a line that should be impossible to say, and to offer a smaller, sadder job. And a tired **Covenant envoy** recognizes West's insignia and asks for help finding a missing courier — the first road toward Elder Vesper, exactly as Tarek's letter urged. Whichever they answer first, the other two keep ticking, and the Combine's recovery team keeps closing.

**So far — and this is the whole campaign in miniature:** two people who were built or bought to serve someone else are learning, slowly, that the only authority left is the one they choose. Enkh will decide whether his debt was ever really the point. West will decide whether they are a person before any court says so. And the sector burns quietly around them, fighting over a voice from under the sea that none of them can understand.

That is where you are. That is what you carry to the table.

# THE GM'S TOOLKIT
## (The practical framework — kept separate from the story on purpose)

*Everything above is the narrative, for memory. Everything below is the machinery, for running the table: the order of play, when to ask for a roll, the fights, and the levers. Use the story to know* why *; use this to know* how *.*

## Session Two — the run order

1. **Cold open — in the black.** A few hours into transit. The berth scene. *(Do not replay the Session One launch; it was played.)*
2. **Transit — one scene each.** Enkh: "The Kid" (West echoes Div). West: the warm locket. Twelve days.
3. **The Reckoning.** Veronika's anger, then the Saint reveal. The emotional center — protect time for it. Default trigger: when a player pushes her, or the moment they read the Tableau.
4. **Arrival — Belum Freeport.** Sector in turmoil, the Tableau on the pier screens. Open the sandbox.
5. **The three doors.** They pick; the others keep ticking. Each has a combat, to exercise the rules.
6. **Close.** Pick a close option by clock and energy.

## The one clock

**The Combine repossession team** lands at Belum on day two to four — your pick. Telegraph it on day two: grey-suited "buyers" at the harbormaster's office, asking about Duskline passengers. They hold a legal writ for West. Paperwork first, force only if forced. Use it to interrupt whatever is running when energy dips.

## When to call for a roll (you flagged under-using checks)

Default difficulties: **8 easy, 10 normal, 12 hard.**

- **Enkh** is good at Notice (a lifetime of casing rooms), Shoot, Sneak and Exert (his cyber-limbs make him strong), and cold transactional Talk. Lean into: reading angles and threats, forcing things physically. Weak at: warmth and charm — that was always Darius's job.
- **West** is good at Shoot and Punch, Notice (threat assessment is literally their function — hand them free "flags" on people and exits), Exert, and tactical reads. Lean into: combat, protecting civilians, planning entries. Weak at: lying under social pressure — West is earnest, and that's interesting.

## The three doors

**Door One — The Quiet Run (Veronika's ask).** She asks them to move six sealed crates, night transfer, "medicine, mostly" — true; it's relief meds and forged refugee IDs, the River Below's real work. *Why they say yes:* she saved them and has never asked for anything; for West it lands like an order. *Main-arc payoff:* the spilled ID wafers are the first hard proof of who Veronika really is, and the pirates who ambush the transfer *bought the Duskline manifest* — the same leak the Combine used to find West.
*Combat:* five Rustwater pirates hit the warehouse airlock. Four pirates — HP 4, AC 12, attack +1, damage 1d6, morale 7. One bosun — HP 8, AC 13, attack +2, damage 1d8, morale 8. Crates give cover. Round three, floodlights: harbor patrol inbound in five rounds.

**Door Two — Small Money (Coyle's job). Run the payment decline first.** The broker Marn Coyle slides the declination across the table: *"Cause of death doesn't match the commissioned work."* Sympathetic, precise, afraid of the client. Pressed hard, his one crack: *"I checked the routing twice and then stopped checking."* Do not explain how the client knew Enkh didn't fire — the only witness was inside his own skull. Then Coyle offers a small job: recover a pawned pretech gyroscope from a ruined ex-caterer, Vess Okaro, for two thousand credits. *Main-arc payoff:* Okaro's other debt was bought by a syndicate quietly buying up the debts of Concordance survivors — someone is collecting witnesses with ledgers instead of guns.
*Combat:* cramped shop, civilians in the crossfire. Three hired guns — HP 5, AC 13, attack +2, damage 1d8, morale 8. The supervisor Rhax — HP 10, AC 14, attack +3, damage 1d8+1, morale 9. Twist: Rhax targets Enkh's cyber-arm and calls him "PALE-job," because his intake list has a *category* for people like Enkh.

**Door Three — The Silent Courier (the road to Vesper).** A tired Covenant envoy, Sister Halex, recognizes West's insignia. A courier, Brother Emmerin, went into the Belum undercity four days ago carrying a sealed dispatch for Elder Vesper and hasn't come out. *Why they say yes:* Tarek's letter said trust Vesper, and Halex is Becoming; she's legitimate Covenant authority, which pulls on West's architecture — potentially against Veronika's hold. *Main-arc payoff:* the dispatch reveals both Covenant factions are hunting the Kael-7 survivors — the Combine by repossession lien, the Covenant claiming its auction title came first. Neither says West is free.
*Combat:* undercity maintenance atrium, two levels of catwalks, hostage zip-tied at the far end. Four Molt gangers — HP 3, AC 12, attack +1, damage 1d6, morale 6. Chief Casque — HP 8, AC 13, attack +2, damage 1d8, morale 8. They break early (morale 6); the real problem is Casque grabbing the courier as a shield.

## If West asks "am I free — who owns me now?"

Do not answer "you're free." The honest answer, from anyone who knows (Halex, a Belum clerk, Coyle): *"Contested. The Combine says you're theirs. Others disagree. Nobody here will settle it or enforce it — so for now you're whatever you decide to be. Careful where you dock."* Three claims (Combine repossession lien, Covenant auction purchase, Senn's weak estate), none of them "West owns West." The arc resolves when West *claims* personhood, not when a court grants it.

## If they go off-script

- Skip all missions and just leave: the repo team lands anyway; Veronika's errand is her reason for being here. The Reckoning plus the repo confrontation is a full session on its own.
- Run from the repo team: it becomes a chase, not a standoff — they're legal-first, not violent.

- Invent a fourth thing: say yes, borrow the nearest stat block, pull the main-arc thread that fits.

## Why is everyone conveniently at Belum? (And what if they want to leave?)

Say this out loud if a player asks — it's the honest answer, not a shrug. Belum is a **Driftborn-served freeport**: one of the hub worlds the trade network actually routes through, light law, no questions at the harbormaster's desk. That is precisely the kind of port people who need to disappear, collect a debt, or move something quietly all end up in — which is why Coyle keeps a franchise storefront there (he keeps one in *every* major Driftborn port, the way a familiar chain shows up in every terminal), and why a Covenant envoy, a missing courier, and an undercity gang are all already living their lives there before the Duskline ever docks. Nobody was moved into place. The heroes walked into a port that was already full of people.

If the table wants to go somewhere else instead, have a real cost ready rather than a hand-wave:
- **Within Belum** (docks, promenade, the undercity) — free, no travel time. All three doors live here.
- **A hop within the system** (an orbital station, a nearby moon) — a few hours to a day by local shuttle. Cheap, easy, doesn't burn the session.
- **Passage to another port entirely** — booking a Driftborn run costs real time, roughly one to four days depending on distance, same as the trip that just brought them here. This is the actual lever: leaving Belum trades directly against **the one clock** — the Combine repossession team, two to four days out. Wanting to chase a lead somewhere else is a real and interesting choice, not a free one.
